Understanding Pick Rate in Draft Analysis

Exploring the assumptions and limitations of pick rate calculations.

Introduction

This section explains how pick rate is calculated and the assumptions behind its interpretation.

Assumptions & Limitations

1. Player Skill Levels Are Not Considered

Some players are better than others - this is true for any competitive game. However, pick rate analysis does not account for individual player skill, as there is no good reason or way to do it.

A strong card that requires high skill may be underrepresented in the data.

2. Assuming All Cards Are Equal

Let's start by assuming that all cards are equally strong and that players pick them randomly. Under this assumption, a card has the following chance of making it into a winning deck:

\( P(\text{card in winning deck}) = \frac{\text{draft_average_occurrence}}{8} \)

And the Pick Rate is calculated as:

\[ \text{pick_rate} = \frac{\text{total_count}}{\text{draft_average_occurrence} \times \text{Number_of_decks}}\]

And in this simplified version for 1 deck, we get \( \text{total_count} = P(\text{card in winning deck}), \text{Number_of_decks} = 1 \)

\[ \frac{\frac{\text{draft_average_occurrence}}{8}}{\text{draft_average_occurrence} \times 1} = \frac{1}{8}= 0.125 \] (12.5% pick rate)

Why This Doesn’t Hold in Reality

  • Equipment: Drafting duplicate equipment has no benefit (other than denial).
  • Class Cards: If a player drafts an unplayable class card, it’s filtered out.
  • Card Strength Varies: Not all cards are equally powerful - this is why analysis is necessary.

What Happens When Cards Have Different Strengths?

Case 1: A Single "Win Game" Card

Imagine a draft where only one card can guarantee victory and all others do nothing. The player who picks it wins.

Pick Rate = \( P(\text{card drafted}) = 1 \) (100%)

Case 2: Two "Win Game" Cards

Now, assume there are two such cards. Each player has an equal chance of drafting them.

\( P(\text{one player has both}) = 0.125 \times 0.125 \times 8 = 0.125 \)

Thus, the pick rate for this card is is:

\( (1 \times 0.875 + 2 \times 0.125) / 2 = 0.56125 \) (56.1%)

Case 3: The Impact of Drawing

Even if a card guarantees a win, it must be drawn during the game.

Assume that a game ends when half the deck is drawn, and if the win card is in the deck, the probability of winning one game is:

\( P(\text{win one game}) = 0.5 + 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75 \)

Winning three games in a row:

\( (3/4)^3 = 27/64 = 0.421875 \) (42.2% pick rate)

Final Thoughts

  • Cards with higher skill ceilings may be undervalued in raw pick rate data.
  • Pick rate is a useful but imperfect metric - additional factors like deck synergy matter.
